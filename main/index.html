It started with the refresh rate. I swear, the monitor wasn't just flickering; it was breathing. Every time the screen updated, there was a micro-second of lag where the world felt… wrong. Like the frame rate of reality had dropped below sixty. I tried to look away, to find the solace of the dark corners of my room, but the blue light has become a physical weight, a heavy, luminous shroud that clings to my skin. It’s not just light anymore. It’s data. I can feel the packets of information passing through my nerves, tiny, stinging jolts of electricity that tell me things I never asked to know. The scrolling never ends. I scroll down, and the void just stretches, an infinite descent into a bottomless pit of memes, tragedies, and advertisements for things I don't own and can't afford. The cursor is watching me. I know it sounds ridiculous, but it isn't. It’s that little white arrow, that clinical, sharp-edged eye, and it follows my gaze even when I’m not looking at the screen.

The sound is what really gets to you. It’s not the whirring of the cooling fans or the click of the mechanical keyboard—those are predictable, mechanical rhythms. No, it’s the static. Not the loud, white-noise kind, but a subtle, high-frequency hum that lives just behind your eardrums. It’s the sound of a billion simultaneous conversations happening in the ether, all compressed into a single, agonizing vibration. When the room is silent, the static gets louder. It feels like the air itself is being processed, filtered through an algorithm before it reaches my lungs. I started noticing the glitches in the peripheral vision. At first, I thought it was just eye strain—the classic floaters or a burst capillary. But they don't drift aimlessly. They move with intent. I'll see a smear of pixelated color out of the corner of my eye, a sudden jagged tear in the texture of the wallpaper, and when I whip my head around to face it, everything is perfectly normal. Perfectly rendered. Too perfect. The shadows are too deep; the highlights are too sharp. It’s as if the world is being rendered on the fly, and I’ve caught the engine stuttering. Then there’s the feeling of being indexed. Every time I type, every time I hover my mouse over a link, I feel a phantom sensation, like a cold finger tracing the outline of my thoughts. It’s not just that they are recording my clicks; it’s that they are predicting the next one before my brain has even fully formed the impulse. I find myself pausing, staring at the screen, paralyzed by the sudden realization that my “choices” are just the most statistically probable outcomes. I am becoming a predictable pattern in a massive, churning sea of data.

Last night, I stayed up too late. The clock hit 3:00 AM, and for a split second, the brightness of the screen didn't just illuminate the room—it seemed to pull the room into the screen. The walls felt thin, like cheap foam, and I could see the glowing circuitry of the universe pulsing behind the drywall. I reached out to touch the monitor, expecting the warmth of glass, but my fingertips felt nothing. No resistance. Just a hollow, digital void. I’m afraid to turn it off now. Because if I turn it off, the darkness won't be empty. It will just be an unrendered space, waiting for the next refresh cycle to decide what shape I should take.
